🔭 European Sovereign Cloud — Strategic Briefing

Date: March 17, 2026 | Classification: Internal — SA Advisory Prepared for: Solutions Architects advising European enterprise customers

Executive Summary

European sovereign cloud has crossed from policy aspiration to operational reality. The AWS European Sovereign Cloud went GA in January 2026. Microsoft completed its EU Data Boundary. The EU Data Act is enforced. And a new Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA) is imminent. Yet the fundamental tension remains: US hyperscalers still hold ~70% of European cloud infrastructure spend, and no enterprise is expected to fully exit them in 2026 (per Forrester). The market is bifurcating into "sovereign-enough" hyperscaler offerings and "truly sovereign" European alternatives — and the definition of "enough" is now a board-level conversation.

Gartner projects worldwide sovereign cloud IaaS spending at $80B in 2026, with Europe as the fastest-growing region (83% growth 2025→2027).

1. EU Policy & Regulation

EUCS Certification Scheme — Still Deadlocked

  • Status: The European Cybersecurity Certification Scheme for Cloud Services (EUCS) remains unadopted as of March 2026. Three assurance levels (basic, substantial, high) are defined, but the sovereignty requirements for the "high" level were stripped from the March 2024 draft after intense lobbying from US industry and some member states.
  • Latest twist: An April 2025 EU Digital Sovereignty draft report proposed re-aligning EUCS "high" with France's SecNumCloud requirements — meaning immunity from extraterritorial laws and EU corporate control. The European Commission is considering using the Cybersecurity Act revision (late 2025/early 2026) to break the deadlock by introducing "non-technical risk factors."
  • Sources: Hogan Lovells, Dutch NCCA, CSIS, CMS Law

🔭 Scout's take: The EUCS deadlock is the single biggest proof that Europe can't agree on what "sovereign" means. The re-introduction of sovereignty requirements via the back door (Cybersecurity Act revision or CADA) is likely. SAs should plan for a world where EUCS "high" eventually does require immunity from non-EU legal orders — it's a matter of when, not if.

EU Data Act — Now Enforced

  • Applicable since: September 12, 2025. Cloud switching rights, data portability mandates, and anti-vendor-lock-in provisions are live.
  • Key deadlines ahead: Data access by design for connected products (Sept 2026), zero switching charges (Jan 2027).
  • Chapter VII requires cloud providers to implement measures preventing unlawful non-EU government access to non-personal data stored in the EU — directly targeting the US CLOUD Act conflict.
  • Sources: European Commission, Latham & Watkins, Skadden

Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA) — The Big One Coming

  • Expected: Q1 2026 proposal from the Commission. Part of the "AI Continent Action Plan."
  • Three pillars: (1) Research & innovation in AI infrastructure, (2) Triple EU data center capacity in 5-7 years, (3) Highly secure EU-based cloud for critical use cases (defense, public admin, critical infrastructure).
  • Key provision: Will define sovereign cloud standards at EU level. Data localization for EU citizen data. Vendor verification for sovereignty compliance. Transition periods for existing services.
  • Legislative timeline: Negotiations 2026-2027, implementation potentially 2027-2028.
  • Sources: European Parliament, Bitkom, EU Cloud AI Act portal

🔭 Scout's take: CADA is the legislation that will actually matter. EUCS was about cybersecurity certification; CADA is about industrial policy. The goal to triple data center capacity is a massive infrastructure play that will create opportunities for every cloud provider. But the "highly secure EU-based cloud for critical use cases" pillar is where the sovereignty battle will be fought.

2. Hyperscaler Response

AWS European Sovereign Cloud ✅ GA

  • Launched: January 15, 2026. First region in Brandenburg, Germany.
  • Investment: €7.8 billion through 2040.
  • Architecture: Physically and logically separate from all other AWS regions. Independent billing, identity, and access management. Operated by a new dedicated company in Brandenburg, subject to EU regulation, managed by EU citizens.
  • Technical isolation confirmed: An AWS engineer on Hacker News stated: "US-based engineers can't access data or operations within the European Sovereign Cloud. All data is really 100% staying within ESC."
  • Trade-off: Limited service catalog at launch vs. standard AWS regions. Debugging issues take significantly longer due to isolation. "Problems that would be fixed in a day or two can take a month."
  • Launch partner: Eviden (Atos) — Eviden KMS for external key management available on ESC as of March 2026.
  • Sources: AWS Blog, Mobile Europe, Eviden press release

🔭 Scout's take: This is real, not theater. The physical and logical separation, EU-resident-only operations, and independent legal entity structure is the strongest sovereignty posture any hyperscaler has shipped. BUT — and this is the elephant in the room — AWS Inc. is still a US company, and the CLOUD Act question hasn't been definitively resolved. For most regulated workloads, the ESC + external key management (Eviden KMS) will be "sovereign enough." For the most paranoid public sector customers, it won't be. The limited service catalog is a practical barrier that will narrow over time but creates a real gap today.

Microsoft — EU Data Boundary Complete, Sovereign Cloud Expanding

  • EU Data Boundary: Completed February 2025. All customer data for core cloud services stored and processed within EU/EFTA.
  • Sovereign Public Cloud: GA across all European Azure regions in 2025. "Data Guardian" feature ensures EU-only storage/processing with EU personnel access control.
  • Microsoft 365 Local: GA in H2 2025. Fully disconnected mode planned for early 2026.
  • Azure Local: Disconnected operations available February 2026. Supports NVIDIA GPUs for air-gapped AI workloads.
  • Foundry Local: February 2026 — run large AI models in fully disconnected sovereign environments on customer hardware.
  • Honest admission: Microsoft acknowledged it "cannot guarantee" EU data will never be requested by US authorities due to the CLOUD Act.
  • Sources: Microsoft Blog, Microsoft Sovereign Blog, Computing.co.uk

🔭 Scout's take: Microsoft's approach is the most layered and pragmatic — they're offering a spectrum of sovereignty from data boundary (lightest) to fully disconnected Azure Local (heaviest). The Foundry Local play for sovereign AI is clever and ahead of the curve. But that CLOUD Act admission will haunt them in every public sector RFP. Their architecture gives customers more choices but also more complexity to navigate.

Google Cloud — T-Systems Partnership Deepening

  • T-Systems partnership extended through 2030 (announced April 2025). Includes supervised cloud (T-Systems operated) and hosted cloud (Google Distributed Cloud, air-gapped).
  • Sovereign Cloud Hub in Munich launched November 2025 — dedicated space for customer engagement, training, prototyping.
  • Google Cloud Data Boundary upgraded June 2025. User Data Shield launched. Expanded dedicated deployments with local operators.
  • Deutsche Telekom itself migrating to Google Cloud sovereign services for its own One Data Ecosystem (ODE) project.
  • Sources: Cloud Computing News, Google Cloud Press

🔭 Scout's take: Google's approach — relying on a European telco operator (T-Systems) to manage sovereignty controls — is the most "European-friendly" hyperscaler model. The DT migration to their own sovereign GCP is a strong reference case. But Google Cloud's European market share remains the smallest of the Big 3, limiting their leverage. The air-gapped GDC option for defense is genuinely differentiated.

3. European-Born Alternatives

OVHcloud 🇫🇷

  • Revenue: €1.085B (FY2025), +9.3% YoY. SecNumCloud ARR at €24M, up 63% YoY.
  • Position: French SecNumCloud leader. Champions European data sovereignty as core brand.
  • Reality check: €24M SecNumCloud ARR on a €1B+ revenue base shows sovereign-specific workloads are still a sliver — most revenue is standard hosting/cloud.
  • Source: OVHcloud FY2025 results

IONOS 🇩🇪

  • Revenue: €895M (H1 2025). Digital Solutions & Cloud at €656M. ~8% growth anticipated for 2025.
  • Position: Explicitly marketing as "European alternative to US hyperscalers." Reports high demand for sovereign cloud products.
  • Source: IONOS investor relations

Scaleway 🇫🇷

  • Revenue: ~$750M estimated (2025). Subsidiary of Iliad Group — no US parent = structurally immune to CLOUD Act.
  • Position: Cost-effective sovereign alternative. Strong on AI infrastructure (GPU cloud). European engineering and hosting.
  • Source: Scaleway 2025 product recap

SAP Sovereign Cloud 🇩🇪

  • Investment: €20B+ to expand sovereign cloud portfolio for European customers.
  • Offerings: SAP Cloud Infrastructure (EU IaaS), Sovereign Cloud On-Site, Delos Cloud (German public sector).
  • AI play: "EU AI Cloud" with Cohere North. Partnerships with OpenAI (via Delos Cloud/Azure) and Mistral AI for sovereign AI solutions — initial use cases expected 2026.
  • Critical context: S/4HANA migration deadline (ECC end of support 2027) is forcing every SAP customer to make cloud decisions NOW.
  • Source: Constellation Research

Atos/Eviden 🇫🇷

  • Position: Launch partner for AWS European Sovereign Cloud. Eviden KMS (European HSM-based) available on ESC from March 2026.
  • Strategy: "Regulatory as Code" — automated compliance templates for DORA, NIS2. Modular sovereign framework across cloud environments.
  • Healthcare: "Cloud for Clinics" platform with AWS for DACH region.
  • Source: Eviden press release

🔭 Scout's take: The European-born players are growing, but let's be honest — OVHcloud at €1B, IONOS at ~€1.8B annualized, and Scaleway at ~$750M are collectively still a fraction of what AWS alone makes in Europe. They're winning in regulated niches (SecNumCloud, government tenders) but not broadly displacing hyperscalers for enterprise workloads. SAP's play is the most interesting because they control the application layer that every European enterprise depends on — the S/4HANA migration wave is a once-in-a-decade moment to shift infrastructure decisions. Eviden's partnership model (sovereignty layer on top of hyperscaler infra) is pragmatic and likely the winning architecture pattern.

4. GAIA-X — The Eternal Promise

  • Status: 380+ member organizations, 12 operational data spaces, GXFS software in production. Trust Framework 3.0 "Danube" released.
  • Showcase: Catena-X (automotive) connecting 1,000+ companies including BMW, VW, Mercedes-Benz. Aerospace and nuclear sectors building on the framework.
  • CISPE commitment: Up to 3,000 Gaia-X Trust-Labelled cloud services by November 2025.
  • Criticism (ongoing):
  • US hyperscalers as members = "Trojan horse" diluting sovereignty mission
  • "Paper monster" — too many frameworks, too few tangible products
  • Agdatahub liquidation (French Day-1 member, €4.8M in funding) illustrates failure to protect the companies it was meant to champion
  • French "Cloud de Confiance" criticized as creating false sovereignty by allowing US tech resold under French label
  • Reddit thread: "GAIA-X is a distraction which should be abandoned" (r/europe)
  • Sources: Gaia-X Summit 2025, CISPE commitment, euro-stack.com criticism

🔭 Scout's take: GAIA-X is not dead, but it's not what it was supposed to be. It's evolved from "European hyperscaler alternative" to "interoperability and data space framework." That's a useful thing! Catena-X is a genuine success. But as a sovereignty vehicle, GAIA-X has failed its original mission. The hyperscaler membership issue was the original sin — it ensured GAIA-X could never be the protectionist tool some wanted it to be. For practical purposes, advise customers to look at GAIA-X for data space/interoperability use cases, not as a cloud procurement framework.

5. Country-Specific Moves

🇳🇱 Netherlands — Your Home Turf, Sameer

  • March 18, 2025: Dutch Parliament passed motions to curb US cloud dependence. Called for:
  • National cloud services platform under full Dutch control
  • Re-evaluation and phasing out of AWS for national domains
  • Preference for European providers in public tenders
  • Cloud Acceleration Team (led by Ron Kolkman) building sovereign cloud marketplace for government.
  • Dutch DPA urged swift implementation of sovereignty criteria for government contracts.
  • Source: NL Digital Government, Licenseware, Euractiv

🔭 Scout's take: The Dutch parliamentary motions are politically significant but practically vague. "Phasing out AWS for national domains" is aspirational — the government runs on Microsoft and AWS, and switching takes years. But the direction of travel is clear: Dutch public sector procurement will increasingly require sovereignty criteria. This directly impacts how you position AWS ESC to Dutch government customers.

🇩🇪 Germany

  • €4B+ invested in digital infrastructure (2025). National Data Center Strategy targeting sustainable, AI-ready, sovereign capacity by 2030.
  • AWS ESC located in Brandenburg. Google Sovereign Cloud Hub in Munich.
  • ICC transitioning from Microsoft to OpenDesk (open-source, German government-funded) — signal of de-Americanization sentiment.
  • Source: Medium

🇫🇷 France

  • SecNumCloud / "trusted cloud" label: French companies can resell US hyperscaler tech under sovereignty label if servers are in France and data is protected from extraterritorial laws.
  • 2026-2030 national cybersecurity strategy emphasizes being a "sovereign digital power."
  • Source: French SGDSN cybersecurity strategy

🇳🇴🇸🇪🇫🇮🇩🇰 Nordics

  • Favor risk-mitigation over strict European-only approaches. Focus on encryption, local backups, compliance measures regardless of provider nationality.
  • Shift toward compliance-driven, AI-native multicloud operating models.
  • Source: Ritzau/ISG

🔭 Scout's take: The Nordic approach is the most pragmatic in Europe — they care about outcomes (data protection, resilience) not flags on servers. Germany and France are the ideological battlegrounds. The Netherlands is pivoting hard and fast — those March 2025 parliamentary motions are the most aggressive sovereign-cloud policy statement any EU government has made.

6. Enterprise Sentiment — Hype vs. Real

What the data says:

  • 60% of CIOs in Western Europe plan to increase use of local cloud providers (2025 survey)
  • Gartner: $80B sovereign cloud IaaS spend projected for 2026
  • 20% of current workloads projected to shift from global to local providers via "geopatriation"
  • 80% of sovereign cloud IaaS spend will come from net-new digital solutions or legacy migrations, not from repatriation of existing hyperscaler workloads
  • Forrester (2026 Predictions): No European company will fully move away from US hyperscalers in 2026 due to cost, integration depth, and feature gaps

What the community says:

  • Reddit/HN sentiment on AWS ESC launch: Mix of genuine interest and deep CLOUD Act skepticism. "Sovereign washing" is a common accusation. Technical users note the limited service catalog as a blocker.
  • Practitioners: The isolation trade-off (slower debugging, fewer services) is a real concern. One AWS engineer confirmed that fixing issues "can take a month" vs days in standard regions.
  • European provider advocates: Strong on Twitter/LinkedIn, but often can't match hyperscaler ecosystems in practice.
  • Sources: CIO.com/Forrester, Gartner, HN thread

🔭 Scout's take: The enterprise demand is real — $80B doesn't lie. But the type of demand matters: most of it is net-new workloads designed sovereign-first (especially AI), not mass repatriation of existing hyperscaler deployments. The Forrester prediction is the most honest assessment: sovereignty is an additive strategy, not a replacement strategy. Enterprises will run hybrid: sovereign environments for regulated/sensitive data, hyperscaler for everything else. This is the architecture you should be advising.

🔭 Scout's take: Defense cloud is where sovereignty stops being theoretical and becomes existential. Google winning the NATO/JATEC classified cloud contract is significant — it validates hyperscaler capability for military workloads. But the EU's own defense cloud ambitions are fragmented by the Cyprus-Turkey impasse. For AWS, the ESC + Eviden KMS + air-gapped options position well for defense-adjacent (but not always top-secret) workloads. The €180M EU Commission tender is a bellwether — whoever wins it sets the procurement template for years.

8. Key Tensions

1. Sovereignty vs. Innovation

The false choice that dominates European cloud debate. Strict sovereignty (EU-only ownership, no hyperscaler) means accepting inferior AI/ML tooling, smaller ecosystems, and slower innovation. Pragmatic sovereignty (hyperscaler with controls) means accepting residual CLOUD Act risk.

Where we are: Most enterprises are choosing pragmatic sovereignty. The "EuroStack" purists are vocal but represent a minority of actual spending.

2. Cost vs. Compliance

  • EU "EuroStack" study (Jan 2026): Claims 60% lower TCO with European providers. But compares baseline infrastructure, not complete workloads with managed services.
  • Data egress fees from hyperscalers remain a pain point. European providers often charge zero egress.
  • Sovereign cloud environments are inherently more expensive due to isolation overhead, smaller scale, and dedicated operations.

The real cost: Compliance itself is expensive — DORA, NIS2, Data Act, AI Act all require tooling, processes, audits. Whoever reduces compliance friction wins.

3. US Hyperscaler Dependence

  • US hyperscalers hold ~70% of European cloud infrastructure market.
  • The CLOUD Act is the single most cited sovereignty concern.
  • No enterprise expected to fully exit hyperscalers in 2026 (Forrester).
  • European providers hold ~15% market share.

4. AI as Sovereignty Accelerant

  • AI workloads are the new frontier — enterprises don't want training data leaving EU jurisdiction.
  • Microsoft's Foundry Local, Google's GDC air-gapped, and AWS ESC are all racing to offer sovereign AI.
  • SAP's EU AI Cloud (Cohere North) and Mistral AI partnership target sovereign business AI.

🔭 Scout's take on the tensions: The market is settling into a three-tier architecture: (1) Truly sovereign (European-only, air-gapped) for classified/defense/critical-national, (2) Sovereign hyperscaler (AWS ESC, Azure Sovereign, Google Sovereign) for regulated enterprise, (3) Standard hyperscaler for everything else. Tier 2 is where 80%+ of the growth will be. SAs should be designing for this tiered model.

Top 5 Strategic Implications for an AWS SA Advising European Customers

1. 🎯 Lead with AWS ESC — It's Your Strongest Card

The AWS European Sovereign Cloud is GA, it's real, and it's the most structurally separated sovereign offering from any hyperscaler. Pair it with Eviden KMS for external key management. For customers who need maximum sovereignty within a hyperscaler: ESC is the answer. But be honest about the limited service catalog and the debugging speed trade-off. Build migration plans that account for service availability timelines.

2. 📋 CADA Is Coming — Start Pre-Positioning Now

The Cloud and AI Development Act will define sovereign cloud standards at EU level. It's expected Q1 2026 with implementation in 2027-2028. Start mapping customer workloads against the three CADA pillars (especially Pillar 3: highly secure EU-based cloud for critical use cases). Customers who prepare now will have a compliance head start. Position ESC as CADA-ready infrastructure.

3. 🇳🇱 Dutch Government Is Your Immediate Risk

The March 2025 Dutch parliamentary motions explicitly called out AWS by name for "re-evaluation and phasing out." If you have Dutch public sector customers, this is urgent. Proactive engagement needed: demonstrate ESC capabilities, sovereignty posture, local investment narrative. The Cloud Acceleration Team is building the government marketplace — ensure AWS/ESC is a credentialed option.

4. 🤖 Sovereign AI Is the Battleground, Not Just Sovereign Infra

Microsoft and Google are both shipping disconnected AI capabilities (Foundry Local, GDC air-gapped). SAP is building an EU AI Cloud. Customers will ask: "Can I train/run AI models on AWS ESC without data leaving?" Position SageMaker and Bedrock availability on ESC aggressively. If there are service gaps on ESC for AI workloads, escalate them — this is where the competitive battle will be won or lost in 2026-2027.

5. 🏗️ Design for the Three-Tier Model

Stop trying to put everything in one region. The winning architecture is tiered: (1) Most sensitive workloads on ESC with external KMS, (2) Regulated-but-standard workloads on EU standard regions with data boundary controls, (3) Non-sensitive global workloads on nearest standard regions. Help customers classify their data and workloads into these tiers. This is consultative selling at its finest — and it's the conversation every CISO and DPO in Europe needs to have.

Bottom line: European sovereign cloud is no longer a policy debate — it's an infrastructure buildout. The money is flowing ($80B in 2026), the regulations are live (Data Act) or coming (CADA), and every hyperscaler has shipped real sovereign products. The winners will be those who reduce the friction between sovereignty requirements and operational reality. As an AWS SA, your job is to prove that ESC delivers sovereignty without sacrificing capability — and to be brutally honest about where it doesn't yet.
